SKYWATCH · USER GUIDE

What is SKYWATCH?

SKYWATCH is a live aircraft tracking and ATC radio frequency advisor built for the airband listener, student pilot and aviation enthusiast. It monitors all aircraft within 100 miles of your home position (default: Leeds Bradford Airport) and uses each aircraft's real-time position, altitude, heading, vertical rate and callsign to calculate the most likely VHF frequencies it is currently using — including the predicted next sector handoff frequency so you can monitor the transfer in real time.

The app covers civil en-route (Scottish Control and London Control), approach and departure radar, ground and delivery, military radar (London Military and Scottish Military), FIR information, LARS and Shanwick Oceanic transition frequencies. It understands the full UK airspace structure including airways boundaries, TMAs, Class G/E airspace, and the Scottish/London FIR boundaries.

Live data: Save this file and open it in Safari or Chrome on your phone. Live aircraft load automatically from public ADS-B networks. Inside Claude.ai the sandbox blocks outbound network requests so demo aircraft are shown — but all frequency calculations work identically.

Screen Layout

  • Left — Traffic List: All aircraft within 100 miles, sorted nearest first. Search by callsign or ICAO hex. Filter by phase using ALL / GND / DEP / CRZ / APP buttons.
  • Centre — Live Map: Top-down aircraft silhouettes rotate to match actual heading. A fading trail shows the last 5 minutes of flight path. A dashed line ahead projects 3 minutes of travel at current speed. Tap any aircraft to open its popup showing current and next frequencies at a glance.
  • Right — Frequency Advisor: Select any aircraft to load the full analysis — route bar, flight stats, reasoning log, and all calculated frequencies ranked by confidence including the NEXT SECTOR handoff card.
  • Header: Live aircraft count · data source badge · UTC clock · ⌖ LOCATION (set home position) · ⚙ API KEY (optional live route data) · ? HELP (this guide).

Home Location

Tap ⌖ LOCATION in the header to change the home position. This updates the map centre, the 100-mile radius circle, all aircraft distances, and re-fetches live traffic from the new position.

  • Use GPS: Taps the browser Geolocation API. On your phone it will ask for permission then acquire your position within seconds. Works in Safari and Chrome when the file is opened locally.
  • Manual entry: Type any latitude and longitude. Useful for a fixed listening spot.
  • Reset: Returns to the default Leeds Bradford (53.7600°N, 1.5942°W).
Your home position is stored in your browser's local storage and persists between sessions. It is never transmitted anywhere.

Map Symbols

  • Aircraft icon colour — flight phase: BLUE = CRUISE AMBER = DEPARTURE RED = APPROACH GREY = GROUND
  • Callsign label — displayed below each icon for quick identification.
  • Fading trail — the solid line behind each aircraft traces its path over the last 5 minutes. Newest segments are brightest; older segments fade. The trail builds progressively across data refreshes (~45 seconds each).
  • Dashed vector ahead — projects the aircraft's current track 3 minutes forward at current speed.
  • White icon (larger) — the currently selected aircraft. Its trail and vector are rendered brighter.
  • Amber dot — your home position.
  • Dashed blue circle — the 100-mile monitoring radius.

Map Popup — Current & Next Frequency

Tap any aircraft on the map to open its popup. In addition to callsign, type, flight level, speed and phase, the popup shows two frequency cards calculated instantly:

  • CURRENT FREQUENCY — the highest-confidence working frequency above 75% for that aircraft at that moment. Shown in blue with sector name and confidence score.
  • NEXT FREQUENCY — the predicted handoff sector, projected 12–35 minutes ahead along the aircraft's current track. Shown in amber. Only appears when a different sector is predicted ahead.

Tap ANALYSE — GET ATC FREQUENCIES to load the full analysis in the right panel.

Flight Phase Detection

  • GND — on-ground flag set, or altitude below 50ft
  • DEP — below 3,000ft climbing, or above 3,000ft with vertical rate above +2m/s
  • CRZ — above 15,000ft with near-zero vertical rate, or any level flight band
  • APP — below 3,000ft descending, or above 3,000ft with vertical rate below -2m/s

Phase is the primary input to the frequency engine — it determines whether Tower, Departure Radar, en-route sector, approach or ground frequencies are most relevant.

Frequency Types

  • ATC — Civil en-route radar. Scottish Control (Prestwick ATCC) covers Scotland and N.England. London Control (Swanwick LACC) covers England and Wales. Each has named sectors with published frequencies. Also includes Shanwick Oceanic for westbound transatlantic traffic.
  • APP — Approach and Departure radar. Active within the TMA of controlled airports, typically up to FL150. Also shown for aircraft transiting another airport's TMA en route.
  • GND — Ground Control and Clearance Delivery. Used at the airport surface for taxi, pushback and IFR clearance.
  • MIL — London Military (Swanwick Mil) and Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil) radar, plus FIR information frequencies and LARS. Available to all aircraft — see Military Sectors section below.
  • VOLMET — Continuous weather broadcasts. UK VOLMET North 126.600 MHz (Manchester, Glasgow, Edinburgh, Belfast, Aberdeen). UK VOLMET South 135.375 MHz (London airports, Amsterdam, Brussels, Paris).

Sector selection: When a position falls within multiple overlapping sectors, a scoring engine uses geographic depth within the sector, altitude band, aircraft speed and heading-to-sector-centre to pick the most likely one. A heading of 315° (northwest) over Leeds at FL300 will correctly score Deancross South over London Upper Middle East, because Deancross South's centre lies ahead on that track.

Next Sector Prediction

When an aircraft is in cruise, the engine projects its position ahead along its current track at its current speed and checks which ATC sector boundary that position enters. The predicted handoff sector appears as an amber NEXT SECTOR card in the frequency panel, and as a NEXT FREQUENCY card in the map popup.

  • 12 minutes ahead — checked first. Finds imminent handoffs.
  • 22 minutes ahead — checked if no change found at 12 min.
  • 35 minutes ahead — checked for FL245+ traffic only, to capture long-range transitions such as Scottish Control → Shanwick Oceanic for westbound transatlantic flights.
Example: AAL195 (London Heathrow → Phoenix) at FL300 over Leeds heading northwest will show Deancross South (135.525) as the current sector and Scottish Upper Central High (132.725) as the predicted next sector in ~12 minutes, then Rathlin East (125.675) on the Atlantic track, eventually transitioning to Shanwick Oceanic (123.950).

Military Sectors — Who They Apply To

London Military (Swanwick Mil) and Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil) provide radar services across the UK FIR. These frequencies apply to all aircraft, not just military types:

  • Below FL075: All aircraft are in Class G or Class E uncontrolled airspace. Airways do not exist here. London or Scottish Military radar service is available to every aircraft — airliners, GA and military alike.
  • In open FIR (more than 40nm from a controlled airport, below FL245): London or Scottish Military provide radar information when no civil unit has jurisdiction.
  • Military callsigns: RRR (RAF), NAF (Royal Navy), RCH (USAF Air Mobility Command "Reach") and other military prefixes are flagged as definitely on military frequencies at any altitude.
  • GA and VFR flights: Light aircraft and flights squawking 7000 below FL150 are almost always in Class G and likely talking to a LARS unit or FIR.

London Military (Swanwick Mil): Central/East/South 135.150, NW/West 127.450, Brize Radar LARS 124.275, London FIR 124.600 / 124.750.

Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil): North ICF 134.300, South ICF 135.925, East 124.050, West 127.275, Scottish FIR 119.875, Leeming LARS 133.375.

The reasoning panel explains exactly why a military frequency appears for any given aircraft — for example: "FL060 — below airways base (FL075). ALL aircraft in Class G/E may be on London/Scottish Military for radar service."

Route Resolution

The route bar shows departure and destination airports resolved in priority order:

  • LIVE — Aviationstack API lookup using the IATA flight number. Returns filed departure, arrival and flight status. Requires a free key (see ⚙ API KEY).
  • SCHEDULE DB — Built-in flight number range database of 75 route entries covering: British Airways (BAW/SHT), easyJet (EZY), Ryanair (RYR), Jet2 (EXS), Loganair (LOG), TUI (TOM), American Airlines (AAL) including AAL195 LHR→PHX, Lufthansa (DLH), KLM, Emirates (UAE), Air France (AFR), Norwegian (NAX), Wizz Air (WZZ), Scandinavian (SAS).
  • INFERRED — Departure only: inferred from position when on ground or departing within 15nm of an airport. Destination heading inference is suppressed for aircraft above FL200 in cruise — high-altitude aircraft transiting the UK are almost certainly not landing at a nearby airport, and guessing incorrectly is worse than showing no destination.

Confidence Scores

Every frequency card shows a percentage with a colour bar. Only frequencies scoring 70% or above are shown:

  • 85–100% (green) — high confidence. Directly matches known sector boundaries or published airport frequency.
  • 70–84% (amber) — moderate confidence. Likely correct but near a sector boundary or handoff point.

MOST LIKELY (blue border) — the primary working frequency. NEXT SECTOR (amber border) — the predicted handoff frequency, always shown regardless of confidence score.

LARS — Lower Airspace Radar Service

LARS is a free radar advisory service for aircraft below FL100, available to all aircraft on request — you do not need to be military or IFR. SKYWATCH surfaces LARS frequencies when an aircraft is below FL100:

  • Leeming LARS 133.375 — Yorkshire and Pennines corridor, operated by RAF Leeming
  • Brize Radar 124.275 — central England, operated by RAF Brize Norton

Squawk Codes

  • 7700 — General emergency
  • 7600 — Radio communications failure. Aircraft follows lost comms procedure.
  • 7500 — Unlawful interference / hijack
  • 7000 — Standard VFR squawk in European airspace
  • 2000 — Entering controlled airspace from uncontrolled airspace
