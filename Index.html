What is SKYWATCH?

SKYWATCH is a live aircraft tracking and ATC radio frequency advisor for the airband listener, student pilot and aviation enthusiast. It monitors all aircraft within 100 miles of Leeds Bradford Airport and uses each aircraft's real-time position, altitude, heading, vertical rate and callsign to calculate the most likely VHF frequencies it is currently using — including the predicted next sector handoff frequency so you can monitor the transfer in real time.

The app covers civil en-route, approach, ground, military and FIR frequencies. It understands UK airspace structure including airways boundaries, TMAs, LARS units, London and Scottish Military sectors, and the FIR information service.

Live data: Save this file and open it in Safari or Chrome on your phone to receive real aircraft data automatically. Inside Claude.ai the sandbox blocks outbound network requests, so demo aircraft are shown — but all frequency calculations work identically.

Screen Layout

  • Left column — Traffic List: All aircraft within 100 miles sorted nearest first. Search by callsign or ICAO hex code. Filter by flight phase using the ALL / GND / DEP / CRZ / APP buttons. Each card shows callsign, ICAO code, aircraft type, altitude, speed and distance.
  • Centre — Live Map: Top-down aircraft silhouettes rotate to match actual heading. A fading trail shows the last 5 minutes of flight path. A dashed line ahead projects 3 minutes of travel at current speed. The selected aircraft is highlighted in white. Tap any aircraft to open its popup.
  • Right column — Frequency Advisor: Select any aircraft to populate this panel. Shows the route bar, flight stats, the reasoning the engine applied, and all calculated frequencies ranked by confidence — including a highlighted NEXT SECTOR card for the predicted handoff frequency.
  • Header: Live aircraft count, data source badge, UTC clock, ? HELP (this guide) and API KEY for optional live route data.

Map Symbols

  • Aircraft icon colour shows flight phase: BLUE = CRUISE AMBER = DEPARTURE RED = APPROACH GREY = GROUND
  • Callsign label — displayed below each icon for quick identification without tapping.
  • Fading trail — the solid line behind each aircraft traces its path over the last 5 minutes. The newest segment is brightest; older segments fade toward transparent. The trail builds up progressively across data refreshes, each roughly 45 seconds apart.
  • Dashed vector ahead — projects the aircraft's current track and speed 3 minutes forward. Useful for seeing which waypoint, airway or airport it is tracking toward.
  • White icon (larger) — the currently selected aircraft. Its trail and vector are rendered brighter to stand out.
  • Amber dot — Leeds Bradford Airport (home position).
  • Dashed blue circle — the 100 mile monitoring radius.

The ANALYSE Button

Tap any aircraft on the map to open its popup showing callsign, ICAO code, type, registration, flight level, speed and heading. The ANALYSE — GET ATC FREQUENCIES button then:

  • Loads the aircraft into the right-hand frequency panel and zooms the map to it
  • Determines flight phase (Ground / Departure / Cruise / Approach) from altitude and vertical rate
  • Matches the aircraft's position against 33 named UK ATC sectors (Scottish Control and London Control) to identify its current sector by name
  • Checks whether the aircraft falls within 14 military sectors (London Military / Swanwick Mil and Scottish Military / Prestwick Mil) based on altitude and position — this applies to all aircraft, not just military types
  • Resolves the departure and destination airports using a three-tier system (see Route Resolution below)
  • Projects the aircraft's track 12–22 minutes ahead to predict the next sector handoff frequency
  • Checks for TMA overflight — if the aircraft is within 40nm of a controlled airport, the local approach frequency is added
  • Inspects the squawk code — 7700 (emergency), 7600 (comms failure) and 7500 (hijack) are flagged prominently
  • Returns all calculated frequencies ranked by confidence with plain-English reasoning for each
You can also select aircraft by tapping a card in the left list, or tapping the icon directly on the map. ANALYSE is only needed when using the map popup.

Flight Phase Detection

  • GND — on the ground flag set, or altitude below 50ft
  • DEP — below 3,000ft climbing, or above 3,000ft with vertical rate above +2m/s
  • CRZ — above 15,000ft with near-zero vertical rate, or any level flight band
  • APP — below 3,000ft descending, or above 3,000ft with vertical rate below -2m/s

Phase drives the entire frequency calculation — a departing aircraft gets Tower and Departure Radar frequencies, a cruise aircraft gets en-route sector and next-sector frequencies, an approaching aircraft gets Approach and Tower frequencies.

Frequency Types

  • ATC — Civil en-route radar control. Scottish Control (Prestwick ATCC) covers Scotland and N.England. London Control (Swanwick LACC) covers England and Wales. Each is divided into named sectors with their own published frequencies.
  • APP — Approach and Departure radar. Active within the Terminal Manoeuvring Area (TMA) of controlled airports, typically up to FL150. Also used for aircraft transiting another airport's TMA en route.
  • GND — Ground Control and Clearance Delivery. Used at the airport surface for taxi, pushback and IFR clearance.
  • MIL — London Military (Swanwick Mil) and Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil) radar services, plus FIR information frequencies. Available to all aircraft — not just military — when operating outside controlled airways. See Military Sectors below.
  • GUARD — 121.500 MHz international distress and guard frequency. Always monitored by all ATC units and aircraft. Shown on every analysis.
  • VOLMET — Continuous weather broadcasts. UK VOLMET North 126.600 MHz covers Manchester, Glasgow, Edinburgh, Belfast and Aberdeen. UK VOLMET South 135.375 MHz covers London airports, Amsterdam, Brussels and Paris.

Military Sectors — Who They Apply To

London Military (Swanwick Mil) and Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil) provide radar services across the UK FIR. A common misconception is that these frequencies are only for military aircraft — they are not. Any aircraft may be talking to a military radar unit depending on where and how high it is flying:

  • Below FL075 (airways base): All aircraft are in Class G or Class E uncontrolled airspace. Civil airways do not exist below this level. London or Scottish Military radar service is available to every aircraft here — airliners, GA, military alike.
  • In open FIR (more than 40nm from a controlled airport, below FL245): When no civil ATC unit has jurisdiction, London or Scottish Military fill the gap and provide radar information or deconfliction to any aircraft that calls them.
  • Military callsigns and types: RRR (RAF), NAF (Royal Navy), RCH (USAF Air Mobility Command "Reach"), and recognised military aircraft types are flagged as definitely using military frequencies regardless of altitude.
  • GA and VFR flights: Light aircraft and flights squawking 7000 (VFR Europe) below FL150 are almost always in uncontrolled airspace and likely talking to a LARS unit or London/Scottish Military.

London Military (Swanwick Mil) key VHF frequencies: Central/East/South 135.150, NW/West 127.450, Brize Radar LARS 124.275, London FIR Information 124.600 / 124.750.

Scottish Military (Prestwick Mil) key VHF frequencies: North ICF 134.300, South ICF 135.925, East 124.050, West 127.275, Scottish FIR Information 119.875, Leeming LARS/Zone 133.375.

The reasoning panel explains why a military frequency appears for any given aircraft — e.g. "FL060 — below airways base (FL075). ALL aircraft in Class G/E may be on London/Scottish Military for radar service."

Next Sector Prediction

When an aircraft is in cruise, the engine projects its position 12 and 22 minutes ahead at its current speed and heading, then checks which ATC sector boundary that projected position falls within. If it differs from the current sector, a NEXT SECTOR frequency card appears highlighted in amber.

This lets you tune to the handoff frequency in advance and listen for the transfer call. The card shows the sector name, frequency and estimated minutes until the aircraft reaches that boundary.

Example: An aircraft on Scottish Control Talla South 126.300 heading south will show London Control North LOREL 129.275 as the next sector with an estimated transfer time.

Route Resolution — Departure and Destination

The route bar beneath the aircraft summary shows the departure and destination airports. This is resolved using a three-tier system in priority order:

  • LIVE — Aviationstack API lookup using the flight's IATA number. Returns the actual filed departure and arrival airports with flight status. Requires a free API key (see API KEY button in the header).
  • SCHEDULE DB — Built-in flight number range database covering 65 routes for major UK operators: British Airways (BAW/SHT), easyJet (EZY), Ryanair (RYR), Jet2 (EXS), Loganair (LOG), TUI (TOM), Lufthansa (DLH), KLM, Emirates (UAE), Air France (AFR), Norwegian (NAX), Wizz Air (WZZ), Scandinavian (SAS) and more. If the callsign and flight number match a known range, the route is identified without any API call.
  • INFERRED — Last resort. Departure is inferred from position when the aircraft is on the ground or departing within 15nm of an airport. Destination uses heading alignment — only suggested if the track is within 20 degrees of an airport bearing, reducing false guesses from the previous wider 35-degree threshold.
No route shown? The aircraft may have an unrecognised callsign, be a private flight, or be a military aircraft without a scheduled route. Add an Aviationstack key for the best coverage.

Confidence Scores

Every frequency card shows a percentage confidence score with a colour-coded bar:

  • 85–100% (green) — high confidence. Position matches a known sector boundary precisely, or a published airport frequency is being used.
  • 65–84% (amber) — moderate confidence. Likely correct but sector boundaries overlap or the aircraft is near a handoff point.
  • Below 65% (red) — lower confidence. Possible alternate frequency, transit situation, or adjacent sector.

The card labelled MOST LIKELY (blue border) is the engine's primary recommendation. The card labelled NEXT SECTOR (amber border) is the predicted handoff frequency — tune to this before the transfer call comes.

LARS — Lower Airspace Radar Service

LARS is a free radar advisory service for aircraft flying below FL100, provided by a number of civil and military radar units around the UK. It is available to all aircraft on request — you do not need to be military or IFR to use it. SKYWATCH surfaces LARS frequencies when an aircraft is below FL100:

  • Brize Radar 124.275 — primary LARS for central England, operated by RAF Brize Norton
  • Leeming LARS 133.375 — Yorkshire corridor, operated by RAF Leeming

Other LARS units exist across the UK (Marham, Valley, Shawbury etc.) — SKYWATCH surfaces the most relevant one based on latitude.

Squawk Codes

  • 7700 — General emergency. All ATC units and aircraft monitor 121.500 MHz.
  • 7600 — Radio communications failure. Aircraft follows lost comms procedures.
  • 7500 — Unlawful interference / hijack. Covert squawk.
  • 7000 — Standard VFR squawk in European airspace (Class G).
  • 2000 — Entering controlled airspace from uncontrolled airspace.
